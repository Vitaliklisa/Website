Game Summary

Tomorrow, and Tomorrow, and Tomorrow follows the decades-long partnership between Sadie Green and Sam Masur, two brilliant game designers who meet as children in a hospital gaming room. Their creative collaboration becomes a vehicle for exploring trauma, identity, and human connection through digital worlds.

The novel spans thirty years of friendship and creative partnership, from their breakout success with Ichigo to later projects that push the boundaries of interactive storytelling. Zevin uses game development as a lens to examine how we construct our identities and relationships in both virtual and real spaces.

About Gabrielle Zevin

Gabrielle Zevin is an internationally bestselling author whose works explore the intersection of technology, creativity, and human relationships. A graduate of Harvard University, Zevin has written numerous novels for adults and young adults, including The Storied Life of A.J. Fikry and Young Jane Young.

Her deep understanding of gaming culture and psychological insight make Tomorrow, and Tomorrow, and Tomorrow a unique exploration of digital creativity as both artistic expression and emotional survival mechanism.

Psychoanalytic Analysis

Trauma & Repetition Compulsion

Zevin roots her protagonists' artistic drive in childhood trauma. Sadie's car accident leaves physical and emotional scars that manifest in her perfectionist coding. Her debugging marathons become Freudian repetitions—attempts to master the uncontrollable trauma through symbolic reenactment in game mechanics.

"Every bug Sadie repairs is a minor triumph over the powerlessness she experienced in the hospital, a symbolic healing tied to her unresolved terror."

Sublimation in Game Design

Sam's design philosophy exemplifies psychoanalytic sublimation. After losing his mother, he channels grief into creating tranquil game worlds with balanced physics and harmonic color palettes. His code transforms raw sorrow into digital serenity, though Zevin reveals this sublimation as imperfect—glitches remind him that control is always an illusion.

Transference in Collaboration

The Sadie-Sam partnership operates through unconscious projections. Sadie seeks validation she projects onto Sam, while Sam draws from Sadie the maternal approval he craves. Their creative disagreements mirror childhood conflicts, making their collaboration an emotional laboratory where past attachments are reworked through game design.

The Digital Mirror Stage

The avatars in Sadie and Sam's games enact Lacan's mirror stage in digital form. Players begin with clumsy characters that evolve into idealized versions, reflecting the creators' own aspirations. Yet glitches in these digital mirrors reveal the fragility of self-idealization, forcing confrontation between imagined and real identities.
